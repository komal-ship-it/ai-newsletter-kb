STARTUP IDEA: AI COPILOT FOR MANAGERS

The Problem: There are 83 million managers in the United States alone. Most of them are winging it. The typical first-time manager gets promoted because they were great at their individual job — which has absolutely nothing to do with running a team. The result: disengaged teams, missed reviews, vague feedback. The global cost of poor management is estimated at over $8.8 trillion in lost productivity every year. That's not a people problem. That's a tooling problem.

The Idea: An AI-powered copilot for people managers to help them become great managers.

Product:
- Connect your calendar and the AI automatically joins every 1:1, captures the conversation, logs action items, and sends you a pre-meeting brief before each session — surfacing what was discussed last time, open items, and suggested talking points based on how each direct report is trending.
- After each meeting, the AI analyses patterns across your entire team — flagging things like "you've spoken 80% of the time in your last 4 meetings with Sarah" or "you haven't given James positive feedback in 6 weeks."
- At review season, the copilot auto-drafts a full performance review for each team member from the year's worth of 1:1 transcripts, sentiment data, and logged feedback — formatted for whatever HR system the company uses.

Business Model: $25–50/month per manager self-serve; $10–30k/year enterprise deals sold through HR teams.

End Goal: Acquired by Workday, BambooHR, Microsoft (Teams), or a talent management platform like Lattice or Culture Amp. Target exit 8–12x ARR.

HOW TO USE GOOGLE STITCH LIKE A PRO

Google Stitch turns plain English into high-fidelity UI designs in minutes. Here's what to do:
1. Go to stitch.withgoogle.com and sign in with your Google account.
2. Before you prompt, define two things: who is using this, and how do you want them to feel?
3. Use the prompt enhancer — it translates vague words like "modern" into specific design language Stitch understands.
4. Tell Stitch your colour hierarchy explicitly: Neutral for the canvas, Primary for key UI elements, Tertiary for your loudest accents.
