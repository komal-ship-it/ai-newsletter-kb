## IDEA OF THE DAY
**Gut health test interpreter with personalized meal planning** — Test To Table
April 3, 2026

A gut health test costs $200 and takes two weeks to arrive. The results: 20 pages of biomarker data, bacterial ratios, and inflammation markers. The report says "increase prebiotic diversity" and "support short-chain fatty acid production." The person holding it Googles three terms, reads two contradicting blog posts, and puts the report in a drawer. The interpretation step has no product.

Test To Table turns any gut health test into a specific diet plan. Upload a results PDF from Viome, Thryve, GI-MAP, or a SIBO breath test. The platform translates biomarkers into plain language, then generates meal plans, supplement protocols, and lifestyle changes ranked by expected impact. Instead of "eat more prebiotics," a user gets "add two tablespoons of resistant starch from green bananas daily for six weeks." A functional medicine practitioner reviews each plan before delivery.

The first build is manual. Analyze 25 gut health reports by hand to map the biomarker patterns that repeat across providers. Use AI to generate initial plans and route each through a practitioner for validation. Ship to 15 users from gut health communities who are sitting on test results they paid for and never acted on.

Pricing at $50 per report for a one-time interpretation or $29/month for ongoing meal adjustments and progress tracking.

## HIDDEN NICHE OPPORTUNITY
**Cat-Specific Telemedicine**

Cats don't show pain the way dogs do. A dog limps. A cat sleeps in a different spot. General vet telemedicine platforms screen for obvious distress signals. A posture shift or a new hiding spot reads as normal. Cat owners use telemedicine to skip the clinic. They need it more than dog owners do. Opportunity: Vet telemedicine trained on how cats show pain.

## FOUNDER PLAYBOOK
**The trigger matters more than the product**

The best customer research has a timestamp, not a wish list. Katelyn Bourgoin ran 300 discovery interviews for her VC-backed startup Vendeve. The conversations were full of encouragement. The company still failed. The core move: interview recent buyers and walk backward through the timeline. Triggers reveal better copy than surveys do.

A freelance marketer named Amy bought Bourgoin's cheatsheets. Real trigger: she'd landed a new client and needed to look competent in the meeting. That shifted the positioning from "learn buyer psychology" to "walk into a client meeting prepared."

The trigger interview script:
1. "Walk me through the moment you decided to look for something like this."
2. "What were you using before?"
3. "What happened that made the old way stop working?"
4. "What would have happened if you hadn't switched?"
