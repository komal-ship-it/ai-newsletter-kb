## Consensus Signals
- **Agentic AI is the dominant paradigm shift** — Covered by Superhuman Code, AI Collective. The "reasoning era" (o1, DeepSeek R1) is winding down; the focus is moving to agents that act over multiple turns using tools, environment feedback, and execution loops. Junyang Lin's essay and the AI Collective's security framing both arrive at the same conclusion from different angles: the agent-as-actor model requires fundamentally different infrastructure and governance than the model-as-oracle model.
- **Claude Code is the week's most-featured developer tool** — Covered by Half Baked (LLM Wiki tutorial), Superhuman Code (Monitor tool, SessionStart hook, Advisor Mode, Cookbook, file search speed). Multiple independent sources pointing developers toward Claude Code as the central tool in their AI-assisted development workflow.
- **AI adoption inside enterprises is broken** — Covered by AI Collective, Superhuman Code (Ramp story). Workers are losing 51 working days/year to technology friction; fewer than 10% are doing meaningful AI work. Ramp's solution (building its own AI suite) and WalkMe's CEO both point to the same gap. This is both a crisis and an opportunity for tooling that reduces friction.
- **Personal knowledge management via AI** — Covered by Half Baked (LLM Wiki), Ideabrowser (Builder Bookmarks, Karpathy's system). The Karpathy-popularized pattern of AI-maintained Obsidian wikis appeared in multiple independent sources on the same day.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Karpathy's "AI Psychosis" framing** — While most newsletter coverage celebrates AI capabilities, Karpathy flags that the people most enthusiastic about AI's capabilities are also the ones most susceptible to overestimating what it can do because of survivorship bias in who pays $200/month. The $200/month user sees frontier performance; casual users see 2024-era ChatGPT and form their opinions there.
- **80% workforce AI rejection** — The AI Collective surfaces data that cuts directly against the "AI is transforming work" narrative: 54% of workers actively bypassed AI tools in the past 30 days. This isn't a training problem — executives and workers are looking at the same software and describing different realities.
